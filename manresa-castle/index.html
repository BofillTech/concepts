I. The Story

One hundred and thirty years on the bluff above the Salish Sea.

The Castle was completed in 1892 as the home of Charles and Kate Eisenbeis — the largest private residence ever built in Port Townsend, thirty rooms in all, with twelve-inch brick walls, a slate roof, and finely crafted woodwork installed by German artisans.

Eisenbeis was elected the first mayor of Port Townsend when the city was incorporated in 1878. Locals called it the "Eisenbeis Castle," its silhouette drawn from the castles of his native Prussia.

In 1927 the Jesuit priests purchased the building for their final year of training. They added a wing housing a chapel and sleeping rooms, installed an Otis elevator, and named the complex "Manresa Hall" — after the town in Spain where Ignatius Loyola founded the order.

The Jesuits left in 1968. Since then, three owners have lovingly restored the building to modern standards while preserving its Victorian elegance. When the Jesuits departed there were three bathrooms in the entire castle. Today there are forty-three.

II. The Rooms

Cosy rooms, quietly restored.

Each room is its own chapter of the castle's history — Victorian moulding, arched alcoves, and the kind of brass and woodwork that simply cannot be reproduced. Modern beds, modern plumbing, century-old bones.
