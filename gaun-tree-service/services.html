Our specialty

Hazardous & Technical Tree Removal

Hazardous removal is what we’re built for. Dead, diseased, structurally compromised, or in a spot where it cannot fall the way physics wants it to. We assess the tree, the property, the access, and the targets — then we plan the removal so nothing else gets touched.

How we approach it

  • On-site assessment of tree health, structure, and lean
  • Mapping of targets — buildings, lines, gardens, vehicles
  • Selection of the right method — climb, crane, or man lift
  • Rigging plan with the proper ropes, pulleys, slings, and blocks
  • Controlled descent of every piece — nothing free-falls
  • Full clean-up — chip, haul, and grind to grade

When we use what

  • Climb Sound wood, room to rig, no immediate threats to climber.
  • Crane Compromised wood, tight quarters, mass that has to come off in one piece.
  • Man Lift Power line proximity, structural lean, climbing the tree would be unsafe.
  • Combination Most complex jobs use two methods — climber on rope, crane on the heavy lifts.
02

Health-first cutting

Structural & Aesthetic Pruning

A good prune doesn’t take more than the tree can spare, and it cuts in a place the tree can heal from. We work with the tree, not against it — protecting long-term form, light, and the resources around it.

What we prune for

  • Long-term structural health and form
  • Crown thinning & selective light
  • Deadwood and broken-limb removal
  • Clearance from roofs, lines, and walkways
  • Aesthetic shaping where it serves the tree

How we cut

  • Proper cuts at the branch collar — never a flush cut
  • No topping, no lion-tailing
  • No more than 25% live wood per session
  • Seasonal timing per species
  • Clean clean-up at end of job
03

Wildfire mitigation

Thinning & Firewise

Northern Arizona is fire country. Pine canopy plus seasonal drought plus the wildland-urban interface — the math doesn’t favor the homeowner. We mitigate the hazard at your property line and at every ring inside it.

Property assessment

  • Walk-through of the entire defensible-space envelope
  • Identification of ladder fuels and canopy continuity
  • Spacing analysis against current Firewise guidance
  • Written scope for the work that’s actually needed

Mitigation work

  • Strategic thinning — keep the trees that should stay
  • Defensible-space buffer zones at 0–5 ft, 5–30 ft, 30–100 ft
  • Crown raising to break ladder-fuel paths
  • Deadwood and slash removal off-site
  • Repeat visits for properties that need ongoing care
