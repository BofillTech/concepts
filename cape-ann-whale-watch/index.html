02 Field Guide

What you'll see on the bank.

Stellwagen draws the largest concentration of whales in the western North Atlantic. Humpbacks dominate the summer log, but on a given day you might also see fin, minke, sei, pilot, and the critically endangered North Atlantic right whale — plus dolphins, harbor seals, and seabirds.

01
Megaptera novaeangliae

Humpback Whale

The acrobat of Stellwagen. Returns each spring to feed on sand lance, herring, and krill. Famous for breaching, fluking, and lunge-feeding through bait balls. Each animal is identified by the unique pattern on the underside of its tail.

Length 48–52 ft
Status Recovering
Frequency Most common
Best Sign Tall column blow
02
Balaenoptera physalus

Fin Whale

The second-largest animal that has ever lived. Sleek, fast, and asymmetric — its right lower jaw is white, the left dark. Travels in small pods, often surfacing at predictable intervals. The blow is tall and narrow, visible from miles off.

Length 65–75 ft
Status Endangered
Frequency Regular
Top Speed 25 kn
03
Balaenoptera acutorostrata

Minke Whale

The smallest baleen whale of the bank. Quick, curious, often solo. White diagonal bands on the pectoral flippers are a distinctive ID mark. Surfaces with a sharp pointed rostrum and a low, almost invisible blow.

Length 23–30 ft
Status Stable
Frequency Common
Behavior Curious
04
Lagenorhynchus acutus

Atlantic White-Sided Dolphin

Travels in tight pods of 50 to several hundred. Frequently rides the bow wave of the Hurricane II — leap, dive, leap again, all in formation. Distinctive yellow and white flank stripe. A reliable highlight on Stellwagen voyages.

Length 8–9 ft
Pod Size 50–500
Frequency Common
Behavior Bow-rider
05
Eubalaena glacialis

North Atlantic Right Whale

Among the rarest large mammals on Earth — fewer than 360 remain. Stellwagen is critical habitat. Identifiable by callosities on the head, V-shaped blow, and absence of a dorsal fin. Federal regulations require careful, distant viewing — and we follow them strictly.

Length 45–55 ft
Status Critically endangered
Population ~360 left
Frequency Rare · Spring
06
+ Other Species Logged

Pilot · Sperm · Sei · Blue

Less frequent but possible: long-finned pilot whales in summer, sperm whales offshore, sei whales (the third-largest baleen), and — once or twice a season — the largest animal ever to exist, the blue whale. Plus harbor porpoises, harbor seals, and Atlantic puffins on the right run.

Range 15–98 ft
Frequency Occasional
Season Late summer
Bonus Seabirds
03 The Place

Stellwagen Bank — a 638 sq-mile undersea plateau, 28 miles east of Cape Ann.

Eighteen thousand years ago, this was glacial moraine. Now it's the most productive feeding ground on the New England coast — a federally protected marine sanctuary where cold, nutrient-rich waters from the Gulf of Maine collide with the warmer offshore current.

Sand lance and herring concentrate here in vast schools. The whales follow. So does everything else: tuna, sharks, seabirds, sunfish. The World Wildlife Fund has named Stellwagen one of the top five whale watching destinations on Earth.

It is one of only fourteen national marine sanctuaries in the United States. Cape Ann Whale Watch has been sailing here since 1979.

07 Plan Your Trip

Before you board.

A few practical things. Whale watching is a working trip on open ocean, not a harbor cruise — dressing for it makes the difference between a magic afternoon and a chilly one.

01 · DRESS

What to wear

  • Layers — even hot summer days are 15° cooler at sea
  • Wind shell or light jacket (essential)
  • Closed-toe shoes with grip
  • Hat that won't blow off, sunglasses
  • Sunscreen — open sundeck, full sun
02 · BRING

What to pack

  • Camera or phone with extra battery
  • Binoculars (helpful, not required)
  • Motion sickness remedy if prone
  • Snacks if you have favorites — galley has plenty too
  • Reusable water bottle
03 · LOGISTICS

Getting there

  • 415 Main Street, Gloucester, MA 01930
  • Free easy parking at the dock
  • 1 hour from Boston, 30 min from Salem
  • Arrive 30 minutes before departure
  • Restrooms onboard, but go before boarding
