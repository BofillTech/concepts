Where are you?

Closer to Havana
than Miami.

Courtney's Place sits 150 miles south of Miami and 90 miles northwest of Havana — closer to Cuba than to the rest of Florida. We're on the highest point on the island: eighteen feet above sea level.

Old Town occupies the western tip of Key West, a roughly four-square-mile knot of cottages, palm-shaded sidewalks, and 19th-century gingerbread. From the lane you can walk to Mallory Square in fifteen minutes, bike to Smathers Beach in ten, or cross to the Gulf side in a leisurely seven-block stroll.

The island isn't big. By car you don't really get anywhere faster than on a cruiser. We rent bikes at the front office — that's how we'd do it.

On Duval (and just off it)

The famous stretch.

A block and a half from the lane — Duval has changed a lot in fifty years, but the good corners are still good. Walk to all of these.

i.

Sloppy Joe's

Duval & Greene · ~3 min walk

Hemingway's old haunt. Tourist-heavy these days, but the burger is still a burger and there's live music every night. Worth a half-hour at the corner of Duval and Greene.

ii.

Capt. Tony's Saloon

Greene St. · ~4 min walk

The original Sloppy Joe's, before it moved. Linda used to run this place. The hanging chair still hangs. Drink rum. Look at the dollar bills on the ceiling.

iii.

Mallory Square Sunset

Wall St. waterfront · ~15 min walk

The nightly ritual. Buskers, sword-swallowers, cats jumping through hoops, and the slow disappearance of the sun. Show up an hour early for a good spot near the railing.

iv.

Hemingway House

907 Whitehead St. · ~6 min walk

Where Ernest wrote To Have and Have Not. The polydactyl cats are descended from his own. The bookcase, the writing studio, the pool — all worth the ticket.

v.

Truman Little White House

Front St. · ~8 min walk

Harry Truman's winter residence for eleven trips between 1946 and 1952. Preserved exactly as he left it, down to the poker chips on the desk.

vi.

Southernmost Point

Whitehead & South St. · ~10 min walk

The buoy with the line out front. Touristy but worth it once. 90 miles to Cuba; bring a friend to take the photo.

On the water

Beaches, reefs, and the blue.

The reef is the only living coral reef in the continental U.S. Don't leave without getting in the water at least once.

i.

Smathers Beach

South Roosevelt Blvd. · ~10 min bike

The longest beach on the island. Wide sand, shallow water, snack carts, paddleboard rentals. Park your bike, swim, repeat.

ii.

Fort Zachary Taylor

Truman Annex · ~10 min walk/bike

The locals' beach. Deeper water, rocky shore, the best snorkeling close to town, and a Civil War fort to wander after.

iii.

Snorkel the Reef

Sand Key, Western Sambo, Eastern Dry Rocks

Half-day trips from the marinas. Coral, parrotfish, the occasional ray. Sea Trip and Fury both pick up at the harbour.

iv.

Deep-Sea Fishing

Charter Boat Row · Stock Island

Marlin, tuna, mahi. Half- or full-day charters out of the marinas. The office can recommend captains we trust.

v.

Dry Tortugas

Day trip by ferry or seaplane

Seventy miles west. A 19th-century fort, untouched water, the kind of snorkeling you remember for years. Worth the full day.

vi.

Sunset Sail

Various marinas · 2-hour cruise

The Mallory Square sunset, but from the water with a drink in your hand. Schooner Wolf, Western Union, and Argo Navis all run good trips.

Eat & drink

Conch, key lime, and the rest.

The local diet is roughly: conch fritters, key lime pie, Cuban sandwiches, fresh-caught snapper, café con leche, and a small ocean of rum. Lean in.

Walk to Blue Heaven in Bahama Village for breakfast — pancakes under the trees, with roosters underfoot. El Siboney on Catherine for the best Cuban food on the island (cash only, no nonsense). Pepe's Café on Caroline for the oyster bar, and Louie's Backyard on the Atlantic for date night.

For sweets, Kermit's for key lime pie on a stick, and Glazed Donuts on Eaton for whatever's coming out of the fryer.

For cocktails: The Green Parrot on Whitehead, Hog's Breath for the live music, and the bar at Better Than Sex when you've earned it. We can point you toward more if you ask at the front desk.

When to come

A few notes on the seasons.

It's eighty degrees year round, so the seasons aren't really seasons — they're crowds and special events.

Jan – Apr

High Season

Mid Dec – Mid Apr · busiest months

Best weather, biggest crowds, highest rates. Book early. The island sparkles, and the bar wait is a little longer than you'd like.

Apr – Jun

Shoulder Season

Apr 16 – Jun 30 · our favourite

Warm but not crowded. Lower rates, easier dinner reservations, water still cool enough to refresh you. The locals' choice.

Jul – Oct

Off Season

Jul 1 – Oct 15 · hurricane window

Hot, quiet, our island again. Travel insurance is a good idea — storms can wander through. Lowest rates of the year.

Late Oct

Fantasy Fest

10 days · last week of October

Costumes, parades, the famous Tutu Race. The island doubles in population. Minimum stays apply, rates go up, book a year ahead.

Nov 11

Parrot Heads in Paradise

Annual Buffett pilgrimage

An annual gathering. Buffett tribute, charity events, lots of Hawaiian shirts. The pool gets busier, the songs get louder.

Dec

A quiet December

Pre-holiday weeks

The first three weeks of December are an underrated sweet spot. Warm, calm, lit up — and we're decorated for the holidays.
